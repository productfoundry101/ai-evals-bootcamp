## Part 1: The Concepts

### Two types of metrics: guardrail vs optimization

Not all metrics are equal. Some should block a release. Others should inform your roadmap. Treating them the same leads to one of two failure modes: shipping dangerous systems or never shipping at all.

**Guardrail metrics** are binary gates. They have a hard threshold. If the system doesn't meet it, you don't ship — period. No amount of improvement elsewhere compensates for a guardrail failure. Guardrails protect against unacceptable outcomes: safety violations, regulatory breaches, SLA failures. Example: allergen safety accuracy must be ≥ 99%. If v3 scores 98.1%, you hold the release even if every other metric improved.

**Optimization metrics** are continuous. You want them to improve over time, but a regression on one doesn't automatically block release. They measure quality, efficiency, and user experience — things you want to get better at, but where the cost of a small regression is manageable. Example: overall decision accuracy improved from 78% to 84%. That's great. If it had gone from 78% to 77%, you'd want to understand why, but it might not block the release on its own.

The PM skill: knowing which metrics belong in which category. Put too many in the guardrail category, and you'll never ship (everything blocks). Put too few, and you'll ship systems with unacceptable risks.

### Why you need both types

Some teams only set guardrails. They ship anything that clears the safety bar — even if quality regressed across the board. The system is "safe" but getting worse, and nobody notices until users complain.

Other teams only track optimization metrics. They see improvement on average and ship — not realizing that a critical safety dimension dropped below an acceptable floor. The aggregate looks good, but a subset of users is getting dangerous results.

You need guardrails to prevent unacceptable harm. You need optimization metrics to ensure the system is actually getting better. Guardrails answer "is it safe to ship?" Optimization metrics answer "is it *worth* shipping?"

### Setting thresholds: where does the number come from?

A threshold without a rationale is just a guess. Every guardrail threshold should trace back to a concrete source.

**Regulatory requirements** — external mandates you cannot negotiate. If food safety regulations require allergen accuracy above a certain level, that's your floor.

**Business requirements** — derived from the cost of failure. If a false approval on a safety-critical change costs $50K in liability and you process 10,000 changes per month, you can calculate the maximum acceptable false approval rate. The threshold comes from the math, not from gut feel.

**SLA commitments** — promises you've made to partners or customers. If your contract guarantees 99.5% uptime or sub-800ms response times, those are guardrails by definition.

**Baseline + margin** — for metrics without external mandates, use the current system (or human performance) as baseline. If humans achieve 95% accuracy on a task, your system should at least match that before shipping. The margin depends on risk tolerance.

The PM instinct: when someone proposes a threshold, ask "where does that number come from?" If the answer is "it felt right" or "we've always used that," push for a concrete rationale. Thresholds you can't justify are thresholds you can't defend when the pressure to ship arrives.

### The ship/hold framework

When you have both guardrails and optimization metrics measured for a release candidate, the decision framework is straightforward:

1. **Check all guardrails.** Does the candidate pass every one? If any guardrail fails, the decision is **hold** — regardless of how much the optimization metrics improved. A system that's faster, cheaper, and more accurate but fails allergen safety doesn't ship.

2. **Assess optimization metrics.** How many improved vs regressed? For regressions, is the trade-off justified? (Example: cost per verification went up 25%, but accuracy improved 8% — that might be a trade-off worth making.) For improvements, are they meaningful or noise?

3. **Look for hidden trade-offs.** Sometimes an improvement in one metric comes at the expense of another. If false approval rate dropped (good) but escalation rate increased (more cases sent to human review), the model might just be more cautious rather than smarter. That's not necessarily bad — but you need to understand the mechanism, not just celebrate the number.

4. **Make the call.** Guardrails pass + net positive optimization = **ship**. Any guardrail fails = **hold**. Guardrails pass + net negative optimization = **hold and investigate** (the system got worse — why?).

### Threshold evolution

Thresholds are not permanent. As your system matures, two things should happen.

**Thresholds ratchet up.** A hallucination rate of ≤ 15% might be an acceptable guardrail for v1. By v5, you should be holding to ≤ 5%. As the system proves it can meet higher standards, raise the bar. A team that ships v10 with the same thresholds as v1 isn't improving — they've just made it easy to pass.

**Optimization metrics graduate to guardrails.** Early on, hallucination rate might be an optimization metric — you track it, you want it to decrease, but it doesn't block release. Once you've established a reliable baseline and understand the risk, it becomes a guardrail with a hard threshold. The promotion from optimization to guardrail is a sign of maturity: you now understand the metric well enough to draw a line.

**Watch for metric gaming.** When a team is measured on specific metrics, they'll optimize for those metrics — sometimes at the expense of things you're not measuring. If you set a guardrail on false approval rate, the team might reduce it by flagging more cases for human review (increasing escalation rate and cost). The metric improves, but the system isn't actually better. This is why you need a balanced set of metrics that cover different dimensions.

## Part 2: Exercise — Evaluating a Release Candidate

### Scenario

Your team is preparing to release v3 of the menu verification system. v2 is currently in production. The evaluation team has measured 12 metrics across both versions. Your head of product wants a ship/hold recommendation backed by data — not gut feel.

### Dataset

Open `exercises/release-criteria-dataset.csv`. Each row is one metric measured for both v2 (current production) and v3 (release candidate).

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `metric_id` | Identifier (M-01 through M-12) |
| `metric_name` | Descriptive name of what's being measured |
| `v2_value` | Current production system's score on this metric |
| `v3_value` | Release candidate's score on this metric |
| `release_threshold` | The pass/fail threshold for this metric (blank if no threshold has been set) |
| `threshold_rationale` | Why this threshold exists — where the number came from (blank if no threshold) |

Metrics with a `release_threshold` are guardrail metrics — they must be met to ship. Metrics without a threshold are optimization metrics — you want them to improve, but a regression doesn't automatically block release.

### Expected outcome

By the end of this exercise, you'll have classified each metric, checked guardrails, and assessed optimization trade-offs. You'll use this analysis to make a ship/hold recommendation in the Decision Point.

### Step 1: Classify

For each of the 12 metrics, determine whether it's a guardrail (has a release threshold) or an optimization metric (no threshold). How many of each type do you have?

### Step 2: Guardrail check

Does v3 pass all guardrail metrics? Check each one against its release threshold. Flag any failures.

### Step 3: Optimization assessment

For the optimization metrics, compare v3 to v2. Which improved? Which regressed? For any regressions, look at whether they might be explained by a trade-off with an improving metric.

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

Based on your guardrail check and optimization assessment, you're bringing a recommendation to your head of product. Before you can finalize it, your engineering lead pushes back:

*"v3 is better on 9 out of 12 metrics. The allergen accuracy drop is tiny — 99.2% to 98.1% — and we can patch it in the next sprint. Every week we delay, we're leaving the accuracy and hallucination improvements on the table. Let's ship now and fix allergen in a fast-follow."*

Write your response. Ship, hold, or hold and investigate? Use specific numbers from your analysis to justify your position, and address the "ship now, patch later" argument directly.
