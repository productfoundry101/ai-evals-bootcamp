## Part 1: The Concepts

### Launch readiness is a gate, not a vibe

"Passes the evals" is necessary but not sufficient for launching. A launch readiness gate is a checklist of conditions that must ALL be met before traffic flips. Missing any one means you're not ready — no matter how good the evals look.

A minimum PM launch readiness checklist looks like:

1. **Offline eval benchmarks passed** — with confidence intervals, not point estimates (D16). CI lower bound must clear the threshold, not just the mean.
2. **Guardrails clean on hold-out** — safety, fairness, and subgroup floors all green on data the model hasn't seen (D10, D12).
3. **Rollback plan exists and is tested** — who pulls the trigger, what command, how fast. If you haven't tested rollback in staging, it doesn't exist.
4. **Monitoring dashboard live before traffic hits** — the leading/lagging indicators from D14 must be instrumented, dashboarded, and alerting *before* launch, not after.
5. **On-call coverage defined** — who gets paged when the first guardrail breach happens at 2am on a Saturday.
6. **Staged rollout plan** — not 0 to 100%. See next concept.

Skipping any of these is a common PM failure mode. "The evals look great, let's just ship" is how teams end up doing post-hoc firefighting instead of planned launch.

### Staged rollouts minimize blast radius

Flipping 100% of traffic to a new version on day one gives you zero time to catch issues before they affect every user. The disciplined PM pattern is a staged rollout with exit criteria at each stage.

Three common stages:

1. **Shadow mode** — run the new version in parallel on real traffic, log its outputs, but *don't serve them to users*. You compare shadow outputs against production outputs offline. This gives you real-distribution test data without any user risk. Typically 1–2 weeks.
2. **Canary** — 1–5% of live traffic routed to the new version. Monitor quality, latency, errors, and guardrail breaches. Usually 3–7 days. If any guardrail trips, auto-rollback the canary.
3. **Progressive rollout** — 10% → 25% → 50% → 100%, with a pause-and-evaluate gate between each step. Pauses let you confirm quality holds at higher volumes before expanding further.

At every stage, define *exit criteria* in advance: what signals mean "proceed to the next stage," and what signals mean "rollback this stage." If your exit criteria are vague ("it looks fine"), the rollout will drift by default toward shipping, even when signals warn otherwise.

### Drift is the silent failure mode — know the three types

A system that passed all offline evals can silently degrade in production. That degradation is called *drift*, and there are three distinct types — each detected differently.

1. **Input drift** — the *distribution of inputs* changes. Users send new kinds of requests your eval set didn't cover. Example: a menu verification system trained on American/Thai/Indian restaurants starts getting Mexican and Korean menus. The model never saw these in training; it might handle them poorly. Detect by: tracking input volume by category over time. Watch for new categories appearing or existing category mix shifting > ~10%.

2. **Output drift** — the *model's outputs change* even on stable inputs. Usually caused by: a model provider updating the underlying LLM, a prompt change that went out without proper regression testing, or retrieval/RAG context shifting. Detect by: pinning a small stable regression set and running it periodically. If outputs on the same inputs change week-to-week, you have output drift.

3. **Concept drift** — the *definition of correctness* changes. Something that was a valid answer yesterday is wrong today. Example: a new state allergen disclosure law takes effect; menus that passed yesterday are now non-compliant. Detect by: tracking ground-truth labels over time, not just model outputs. When human reviewers start flagging things they used to approve, the concept changed.

The three matter because the fix is different for each. Input drift → expand your eval set and possibly retrain. Output drift → pin model versions and re-run regressions before any change. Concept drift → update your golden dataset ground truth first, then re-evaluate your system against the new standard.

### Online evals: how you continuously evaluate production traffic

You cannot hand-grade every production request. You also cannot ignore production traffic and only trust pre-launch evals. The solution is a layered online-eval strategy:

1. **100% lightweight automated checks** — fast, cheap rule-based checks on every request (JSON schema, profanity filters, PII detectors, hard safety blocks). These are guardrails, not quality measurements. D5 called these "code-based graders."

2. **Stratified sampling for deeper checks** — sample 1–5% of production traffic, biased toward interesting segments (edge cases, flagged responses, new input categories, subgroups with low volume). Run LLM-as-judge (D6) on this sample. This is your continuous quality signal.

3. **Human review of a smaller slice** — take 20–50 traces per week from the LLM-judge sample, grade manually, and use the agreement/disagreement to recalibrate the judge. This is your meta-evaluation loop (D6).

4. **Feedback loop into golden dataset** — production failures found through sampling become new cases in your golden dataset (D7). Without this loop, your golden dataset grows stale and stops catching what production now produces.

The PM discipline: **online eval coverage should be decided by risk, not by budget.** A safety-critical subgroup with low volume (e.g., allergen-sensitive Thai cases) needs deeper sampling than the high-volume easy cases, even though it costs more per sample.

### Rollback criteria must be set in advance, with owners

If you haven't defined *what triggers rollback* before launch, you will not rollback when you should. You'll rationalize every warning sign as "noise" because the humans currently in the room have too much ego invested in the shipped version.

A rollback plan has three parts:

1. **Numerical triggers** — specific thresholds that, if breached, force a rollback conversation. Example: *aggregate quality drops >5pp below pre-launch baseline for two consecutive weeks*, or *any safety guardrail breach in a 24h window*, or *p95 latency >SLO for >15 min*. These must be written down, not verbal agreements.

2. **Decision owner** — *who* decides to pull the trigger when a threshold is breached. For most product decisions this is the PM, sometimes with eng lead. For safety breaches, it's often automatic (the system rolls back itself) or has a very short escalation chain.

3. **Rollback mechanics** — *how*, *how fast*, and *what happens to in-flight requests*. A rollback that takes 4 hours to execute is useless for a safety incident. Rollback must be tested in staging.

One subtle but important point: rollback is not always "revert to the old version." Sometimes the right rollback is to keep the new version but add a guardrail, or to route only the affected subgroup back to the old version. Your rollback plan should cover more than one path.

## Part 2: Exercise — Six Weeks of Production Monitoring

### Scenario

You shipped the v2 menu verification prompt from D16 (after fixing the American cuisine regression with additional training cases, which cleared the guardrail on the hold-out set). The system went live under a progressive rollout: 10% Week 1, 50% Week 2, 100% from Week 3 onward.

It's now six weeks post-launch. The CEO asks you: *"How's the new system doing?"* Engineering says *"Looks fine, aggregate quality is steady."* Your CTO forwards you a customer complaint about a Mexican menu approving a shellfish dish with no disclosure. You need to investigate before answering either of them.

### Dataset

Open `exercises/production-monitoring-dataset.csv`. Each row is one week of production monitoring data.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `week` | W1 through W6 (weeks since launch) |
| `week_start` | Start date of the monitoring week |
| `model_version` | Which model version was serving traffic that week |
| `total_requests` | Total menu verification requests that week |
| `thai_pct`, `american_pct`, `indian_pct`, `other_pct` | Cuisine mix by volume share (%) |
| `new_cuisines_seen` | Any new cuisines appearing in traffic that week (not in eval set) |
| `allergen_guardrail_breaches` | Confirmed allergen-disclosure failures that reached users |
| `p95_latency_ms` | 95th percentile response latency |
| `llm_judge_pass_rate` | LLM-as-judge score on ~1000 sampled requests |
| `human_review_pass_rate` | Human review pass rate on ~50 sampled requests |
| `notes` | Any operational notes (model updates, regulatory changes, etc.) |

### Expected outcome

By the end: (1) you'll have assessed whether launch readiness criteria were met, (2) classified the type(s) of drift occurring, (3) decided whether rollback criteria have been breached, and (4) proposed a specific action (hold steady, rollback, or targeted fix).

### Step 1: Establish the baseline and look at the trend

Look at Week 1 (launch week). Record the following five baseline values:

1. `llm_judge_pass_rate` — your automated quality signal
2. `human_review_pass_rate` — your ground-truth quality signal
3. `allergen_guardrail_breaches` — your safety signal (pre-committed hard guardrail: 0 per week)
4. `p95_latency_ms` — your performance signal (pre-committed SLO: ≤ 1000ms)
5. Cuisine mix (`thai_pct`, `american_pct`, `indian_pct`, `other_pct`) — your input distribution baseline

Now scan the same five signals across all six weeks. For each: is it stable, drifting gradually, or shifting suddenly? At which week does each signal first deviate meaningfully from baseline?

**Key question:** the LLM-judge score and human review score started close together at launch. Do they stay close? If not, what does that divergence tell you?

### Step 2: Classify what kind of drift is happening

Using the three drift types from Part 1 — input drift, output drift, concept drift — look for evidence of each in the data.

- For **input drift**: look at the cuisine mix columns and `new_cuisines_seen`. Is the input distribution shifting? When?
- For **output drift**: look at `model_version` and `notes`. Did anything change about the model serving traffic?
- For **concept drift**: look at `notes` for any regulatory or ground-truth changes.

For each type you identify, pinpoint the week it starts and describe what evidence proves it.

**Key question:** can you have more than one drift type happening at the same time? If so, does that make the root cause harder or easier to isolate?

### Step 3: Check the guardrails

Using the D10 framework for setting pre-committed thresholds, the following guardrails were established before v2 launched:
- Allergen breaches: **0 per week** (hard guardrail — safety critical)
- Aggregate human review pass rate: **≥ 75%** (quality threshold)
- p95 latency: **≤ 1000ms** (performance SLO)

Which guardrails are breached, and in which weeks? Count breaches by week. What does the breach pattern tell you about when the system first failed its own ship criteria?

**Key question:** if the allergen breaches first appeared in W4 and you're in W6 now, how long has the system been shipping past its own safety threshold?

### Step 4: Apply rollback criteria and recommend an action

Pre-launch you also set the following rollback triggers:
- **Safety trigger:** any allergen breach in a rolling 4-week window → immediate investigation; 2+ breaches in 4 weeks → rollback.
- **Quality trigger:** human review pass rate drops ≥ 7pp below baseline for 2 consecutive weeks → rollback conversation.
- **Performance trigger:** p95 latency > SLO for > 2 weeks → rollback conversation.

Based on your analysis, which triggers have been breached by W6?

Now propose an action. Your options:
1. **Hold steady** (keep v2.1 live, no changes)
2. **Full rollback** (revert to the old v1 prompt)
3. **Targeted fix** (specify: what exactly, for which subgroup, and what criteria must be re-hit before re-expanding)

Whichever you pick, you must state: *why this action, not the other two.*

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

It's Monday morning, Week 7. You're in the weekly AI review with your CTO and eng lead. Engineering opens with:

*"The aggregate numbers have been steady-ish. Yes, we saw a few allergen breaches, but those were all on Mexican menus — we didn't have Mexican in our eval set, so of course the model struggles. And the latency blip in Week 5 was from the underlying model provider pushing an update; that's out of our hands. Not a quality issue with our system. Let's keep v2.1 running and add Mexican training data for the next iteration."*

Your response should do three things:

1. **State your action recommendation** — hold, full rollback, or targeted fix. Name specific weeks, numbers, or subgroups you're relying on.

2. **Identify at least two things engineering got wrong or glossed over** in their framing. Use the drift taxonomy and the rollback criteria to be specific. Hint: "out of our hands" is almost never true in an AI system a PM owns.

3. **Propose the monitoring or launch-readiness gap** that allowed the system to be shipping past its own guardrails for multiple weeks without anyone acting. Don't just say "better monitoring" — name the specific missing mechanism (a missing alert, a missing rollback ownership, a missing regression suite on model-provider updates, a missing eval coverage for input drift, etc.).

Don't frame this as an abstract critique. You are the PM who set the launch readiness gates in the first place. If a gate was missing, it's your miss to own and fix.
