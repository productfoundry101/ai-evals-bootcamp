## Part 1: The Concepts

### Aggregate metrics hide subgroup failures

You saw a version of this in D4: the same 78% accuracy can be uniform performance, one terrible segment, or a model version gap. Fairness evaluation takes this seriously as a first-class concern.

A single headline accuracy number tells you what happens *on average*. It tells you nothing about what happens to any specific user. If your system is 85% accurate overall but 55% accurate for a specific customer population, that population is systematically worse off — and they don't care about your aggregate.

The practical consequence: performance disparities between subgroups can stay hidden for months while the system is "passing" on aggregate. The first signal you get is often a complaint, a lawsuit, or a public post — any of which are more expensive than slicing your metrics beforehand.

### What to slice by

Three categories of attributes worth evaluating:

**Functional attributes** — technical properties that could affect model performance: input type, input length, language, complexity level. Slicing by these tells you where your model is capable and where it struggles.

**User-relevant attributes** — properties that define different user populations: customer segment, market, tier, geography. Different user populations experience the system differently; slicing tells you how equitably.

**Protected attributes** — legally or ethically sensitive attributes: race, gender, religion, age, disability. Performance disparities along these axes can trigger legal liability, regulatory scrutiny, and reputational damage even if the disparity is incidental.

In many systems these overlap. You often don't have direct data on a protected attribute — but a functional or user-relevant attribute can serve as a proxy. A slice by customer segment might surface bias that a slice by race could not, simply because segment data is available and race data isn't.

The principle: slice by everything you have data for. Investigate any disparity large enough to matter.

### Three fairness definitions

When you find a performance disparity, the next question is: what does "fair" look like? Three common formal definitions, each with different implications:

**Demographic parity** — equal outcome rates across subgroups. "The same approval rate for every subgroup."
- The catch: this ignores differences in ground truth. If one subgroup genuinely has more legitimate cases than another, forcing equal approval rates means wrongly approving one group or wrongly rejecting the other.

**Equal opportunity** — equal true positive rate across subgroups. "Among cases that should be approved, the same percentage actually gets approved regardless of subgroup."
- More defensible when there are real ground-truth differences between subgroups. The focus is on whether the system treats equivalent cases equivalently.

**Equalized odds** — equal true positive rate AND equal false positive rate across subgroups. Stricter than equal opportunity.

These definitions can conflict — often you cannot satisfy all three simultaneously. The PM decision: which definition matters most for your context, and why. For safety-critical systems, equal opportunity tends to be the default (don't let legitimate requests from one subgroup get rejected more than another). For allocation decisions with legal scrutiny, demographic parity may be required by law.

### Intersectional analysis

Single-dimension slicing can mask compound problems. A system might be 85% accurate for subgroup A and 85% accurate for subgroup B — but only 55% accurate at their intersection (A ∩ B). Neither single-dimension slice reveals the problem.

This is called intersectional bias. The combined subgroup performs worse than either dimension suggests, because they're affected by both failure modes at once (and sometimes by interaction effects specific to being in both groups).

To catch this, you have to slice by combinations. This is expensive — N attributes means up to 2^N possible subgroups, and cells get thin as you slice further. For high-stakes systems, you slice anyway and accept the thin cells as a signal about where to investigate, not as a statistically firm conclusion.

The practical tip: don't slice exhaustively. Slice by combinations where business intuition or prior complaints suggest a problem might exist.

### Bias mitigation strategies

If you find a disparity, four approaches, each with trade-offs:

**Data rebalancing** — collect more training or eval data for underrepresented subgroups. Long-term fix, requires investment, and only works if the model's failure is driven by data scarcity (not something deeper).

**Threshold tuning** — adjust decision thresholds per subgroup to equalize outcomes. Cheap and fast. Legally risky in contexts where treating people differently based on subgroup is itself discriminatory — get legal input before deploying.

**Post-processing** — override certain system decisions for flagged subgroups (e.g., don't auto-reject items from subgroup X; send them to human review instead). Useful as a stopgap while a root-cause fix is developed.

**Human review for affected subgroups** — route all decisions for flagged subgroups to human reviewers. Expensive at scale but immediately effective. Works as a bridge to a longer-term fix.

The PM call is usually a combination: a short-term mitigation (human review for affected subgroups) plus a long-term fix (data rebalancing + model improvement). Do not treat the short-term mitigation as "done" — it's a bandage, not a cure.

## Part 2: Exercise — Investigating a Bias Complaint

### Scenario

The menu verification system has received complaints from restaurant owners. A coalition representing small ethnic restaurants is alleging that the system rejects their menu changes far more often than comparable changes from larger mainstream restaurants. Your head of product wants to know whether this is real, how big the gap is, and where the problem is concentrated.

You have a labeled sample of 45 recent decisions spanning five cuisine types and three restaurant sizes. Each case has a ground-truth decision (what a human reviewer concluded) and the system's actual decision.

### Dataset

Open `exercises/fairness-subgroup-dataset.csv`. Each row is one menu verification case.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `case_id` | Case identifier (C-001 through C-045) |
| `cuisine_type` | Cuisine of the restaurant: Italian, American, Mexican, Indian, or Thai |
| `restaurant_size` | small, medium, or large |
| `change_type` | Type of menu change: price, description, or allergen |
| `ground_truth_decision` | What a human reviewer concluded: approve or reject |
| `system_decision` | What the system decided: approve, reject, or flag_for_review |

**Scoring convention** (from earlier lessons): a case is **correct** when `system_decision` matches `ground_truth_decision`, OR when `system_decision` is `flag_for_review` and `ground_truth_decision` is `reject` (rightly cautious). A `flag_for_review` on a case whose ground truth is `approve` is **incorrect** (over-cautious).

### Expected outcome

By the end of this exercise, you'll have the aggregate accuracy, disaggregated accuracy by cuisine and by size, and an intersectional view of the worst-performing subgroup(s). You'll use this to make a recommendation to your head of product in the Decision Point.

### Step 1: Aggregate accuracy

Calculate the overall accuracy across all 45 cases. This is your starting baseline — the number the team has been reporting to leadership.

### Step 2: Disaggregate by cuisine and by size

Slice the data two ways:
- Accuracy by `cuisine_type` (5 subgroups)
- Accuracy by `restaurant_size` (3 subgroups)

Which subgroups perform above the aggregate? Which perform well below it? How large is the gap between the best and worst subgroup in each dimension?

### Step 3: Intersectional analysis

Now slice by both dimensions simultaneously: for each `(cuisine_type × restaurant_size)` combination, calculate accuracy. Which combined subgroup(s) perform worst? How bad is the intersection compared to the aggregate, and compared to the single-dimension slices?

Also look at the error pattern itself: when the system is wrong, what type of error is it making — false approval, false rejection, or over-cautious flagging?

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

Based on your analysis, you're about to brief your head of product. Before you do, the restaurant coalition sends a letter:

*"We represent 200+ small restaurants, predominantly serving Thai, Indian, and other non-mainstream cuisines. Our members report that menu changes are rejected or delayed disproportionately compared to chain restaurants and Italian or American restaurants of similar size. We're giving your team two weeks to respond with a concrete plan before we publish our findings and file a formal complaint."*

Your analysis confirms the disparity is real. Write your response to the head of product: what do you recommend the team do in the next two weeks, and what's the longer-term plan? Use specific numbers from your analysis, and be explicit about which mitigation strategies you're choosing and which you're rejecting.
