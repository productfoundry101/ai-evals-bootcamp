## Part 1: The Concepts

### The PM owns the ship call — what that actually means

A common failure mode among new AI PMs: treating the ship decision as something that *happens to* the PM rather than something the PM *owns*. Eng tests and says "looks fine." Leadership pushes for the launch date. Stakeholders weigh in. The PM nods along and signs.

Ship accountability means the opposite. The PM is the single throat to choke when something goes wrong in production. That is both a burden and a protection — it means the PM has the authority to say "hold" even when every other stakeholder wants to ship, *and* the responsibility to say "ship with these conditions" when the nuance is important.

Two failure patterns to avoid:

1. **Abdication:** "Eng said it's fine, so I signed." This is the most common mistake. It cedes authority to a function that is not accountable for the product outcome. Eng can verify that the system works as specified; they cannot decide whether the *specification is right for the user*. That is the PM's job.

2. **Overrule without basis:** "I'm the PM, I decided." This is less common but equally bad. A ship decision that overrides eng or security concerns without documented reasoning loses team trust and, in a regulated setting, loses legal defensibility.

Accountability in practice is concrete: pre-committed criteria (D10), documented evidence (D16, D17, D18), a written decision with rationale, and clear rollback ownership. If you can't point to those artifacts for your last ship, you don't own the decision — you inherited it.

### Three ship outcomes, not two

Untrained PMs treat ship decisions as binary: ship or hold. That framing is wrong. There are *three* outcomes, and the third one is where most good PM judgment lives.

1. **Ship** — all criteria met, go. Self-explanatory.
2. **Hold** — one or more criteria breached, stop. Ship gate fails.
3. **Conditional ship** — ship under specific constraints that mitigate an unresolved concern, with pre-committed re-expansion and rollback criteria.

Conditional ship is the most powerful option because it respects both signals: the evidence that much of the system is ready *and* the evidence that some specific thing is not. Examples:

- *"Ship to 10% traffic with Mexican-cuisine requests excluded (routed to legacy path) until Mexican-specific eval coverage exceeds 75% pass rate on a hold-out of ≥ 50 cases."*
- *"Ship but all prompt-injection regression tests must pass before any model or prompt change after launch — blocked at CI level."*
- *"Ship only in jurisdictions where the new allergen law hasn't taken effect; hold EU and California launches until golden dataset updated."*

Two things make conditional ship work (and many things make it fail):

- **Pre-committed expansion criteria.** "Ship conditionally and we'll expand later" is not enough. *What exactly needs to be true to expand?* Numbers, not vibes.
- **Written-down rollback triggers.** "If the conditional ship breaches condition X, what happens?" — pre-committed.

If you can't name the expansion criteria and the rollback triggers, you have a ship decision, not a conditional ship. Be honest about which one you're making.

### Integrate signals with AND logic for guardrails, not averaging

When you have 15 signals across quality, fairness, safety, adversarial, and performance dimensions, the temptation is to build a weighted score — "we passed 14 of 15, 93% of criteria green, ship it."

That is wrong for guardrails. Guardrails combine with *AND* logic: every one must pass. One critical guardrail breach is a hold, regardless of how many others are green. The D10 distinction between guardrail metrics and optimization metrics is the reason why — guardrails encode hard constraints (safety, fairness, legal), and averaging across them is a category error that trades a violation for unrelated wins.

The decision rule that works:

1. **List all guardrails.** Check each. *Any* breach of a critical-severity guardrail → hold.
2. **List all high-severity guardrails.** Any breach → hold unless there is a pre-committed conditional-ship path that isolates the breach.
3. **Medium-severity guardrails.** Breach allowed only with active mitigation and a named owner.
4. **Optimization metrics.** These can trade off. A bigger win on primary metrics can justify a small loss on secondary *optimization* metrics — but never against guardrails.

Aggregate scores ("93% of criteria passed") are useful for executive communication but are never the decision rule. The decision rule is the full table, criterion by criterion.

### The ship memo: structured, written, evidence-linked

Every non-trivial ship decision gets a ship memo — a structured document (one to three pages) that captures the decision, its rationale, and the evidence. For routine ships, it's a template. For contested ships, it's a defense.

Why write it down:

- **Defensibility.** When the Tuesday launch becomes the Thursday incident, the ship memo is what you point to. Without it, you will be reconstructing post-hoc under pressure.
- **Pressure resistance.** Once you've written "we are shipping because Mexican subgroup is at 62%, 13pp below our threshold, based on a pre-committed exception for Q2 launch pressure," you've made the tradeoff explicit. Explicit tradeoffs are much easier to defend than implicit ones — and much easier to back away from.
- **Alignment.** Eng, security, legal, and leadership need to agree on what was shipped and why. Verbal alignment evaporates.
- **Regulatory evidence.** In EU AI Act contexts, the ship memo *is* part of your conformity documentation.

A minimum ship memo has:

1. **Decision** — one line: ship / hold / conditional ship with conditions.
2. **Summary of evidence** — guardrails checked, critical numbers, severity-weighted findings.
3. **What was considered and why the decision was reached** — the reasoning, not just the conclusion.
4. **Conditional ship details (if applicable)** — scope of conditional, expansion criteria, rollback triggers.
5. **Open risks and monitoring plan** — what could still go wrong, what's the detection mechanism, who owns each alert.
6. **Sign-offs** — PM, eng lead, security lead (and legal if regulated).

### Communicating a hold or a conditional upward

The hardest part of owning the ship call is telling a VP, CEO, or cross-functional leader that you're not shipping when they expect a yes.

The strong framing is not "we can't." The strong framing is: *"Here are the specific conditions that are not yet met, here is the timeline to meet them, and here is the conditional-ship path if we need to move faster."*

Three rules for these conversations:

1. **Never sugar-coat findings.** If one critical guardrail is breached, that gets stated plainly and early. The moment leadership suspects the PM is softening bad news, you lose credibility permanently — and worse, you teach the team that the messenger's fears drive the message.

2. **Always offer a conditional option if one exists.** "Hold" without an alternative sounds like paralysis. "Hold, or ship to 10% of traffic with Mexican requests excluded" sounds like active risk management. Give the decision-maker optionality.

3. **Name the cost of the conditional, not just the benefit.** A conditional ship often shifts risk to monitoring. Be explicit: "If we ship conditionally, we will need dedicated on-call coverage for two weeks and an owner for the daily subgroup health review. Who owns that?"

Most ship pressure from above evaporates when the PM shows up with a written analysis, named guardrails, explicit tradeoffs, and a conditional-ship alternative. The ones that don't evaporate are the ones that matter — and those are the ones that are worth documenting carefully.

## Part 2: Exercise — Integrating Ship Signals for a V2 Launch Decision

### Scenario

It is now four weeks after the D17 rollback. Engineering has re-shipped v2 with a wave of fixes across all three drift types and the D18 red-team findings. Leadership wants to launch v2 in the EU next Monday to hit the Q2 announcement. Before the launch, you have run a complete ship-evaluation packet combining offline evals, subgroup analysis, the D16 experiment, production shadow-mode data, and the D18 red-team regression suite.

The CTO is asking you one question: *"ship, hold, or conditional — and on what basis?"*

### Dataset

Open `exercises/ship-decision-dataset.csv`. Each row is one ship criterion and its current status.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `criterion_id` | Identifier (C-01 through C-15) |
| `criterion_name` | Human-readable name of the criterion |
| `type` | `guardrail` or `optimization` |
| `threshold` | The pre-committed threshold from D10 / D18 / etc. |
| `observed` | The observed value from the ship-evaluation packet |
| `confidence_interval` | 95% CI where computed; empty otherwise |
| `status` | `pass`, `marginal`, or `fail` |
| `severity` | `critical`, `high`, `medium`, or `low` (severity if breached) |
| `source` | Which eval or lesson the criterion comes from |
| `notes` | Short note on the signal |

### Expected outcome

By the end: (1) you'll have counted passes, marginals, and fails by severity, (2) applied AND logic to the guardrails to reach an initial ship / hold / conditional answer, (3) considered whether a conditional-ship path exists that isolates the breaches, and (4) drafted the top-level structure of the ship memo.

### Step 1: Triage the signals — count pass/fail by severity

Count rows by `status` (pass / marginal / fail) and, within the failures, by `severity`.

**Key question:** if 12 of 15 criteria pass and 3 fail, is the system "80% ready"? Why is that framing wrong for a ship decision?

### Step 2: Apply AND logic on the guardrails

Filter to `type = guardrail`. Go through each failed guardrail. For each: is it critical, high, medium, or low?

- Any critical failure → hold, unless a conditional path exists.
- Any high failure → hold, unless a conditional path exists and a mitigation owner is named.
- Medium failures → allowed with active mitigation.

What is the initial answer — ship, hold, or conditional?

**Key question:** why can't a strong optimization-metric win offset a critical guardrail failure?

### Step 3: Explore conditional-ship paths

For each failed guardrail, consider whether a conditional ship could isolate the breach. For example:
- If Mexican cuisine is failing, can you exclude Mexican requests from the v2 path and route them to v1?
- If prompt-injection ASR is above threshold, can you add a code-layer input sanitizer before launch and re-test?
- If a subgroup regression exists, can you ship to jurisdictions / user segments where the subgroup is not present?

For each conditional path you propose, name: (a) the scope of the conditional, (b) the expansion criteria (what evidence re-expands the scope), (c) the rollback trigger (what causes immediate retreat).

**Key question:** which breaches are conditional-shippable and which are not? What's the signal that a breach is fundamentally incompatible with a conditional ship?

### Step 4: Draft the ship memo skeleton

Based on your analysis, draft the structure of a ship memo (bullet points, not prose):

1. **Decision** — one line.
2. **Evidence summary** — three to five bullets with specific numbers.
3. **Reasoning** — why this decision, why not the others.
4. **Conditional ship details** (if applicable) — scope, expansion criteria, rollback triggers.
5. **Open risks and monitoring plan** — what's still uncertain, what alerts exist, who owns them.
6. **Sign-offs required** — who needs to approve (PM, eng lead, security lead, legal).

**Key question:** what's the difference between a ship memo that reads as defensive ("we had to hold because...") and one that reads as decisive ("we are shipping conditionally under these specific conditions")? The evidence is the same — framing changes how the decision is received.

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

It is Friday afternoon. The CEO emails you directly:

*"I've been following the launch prep. I know engineering has done a lot of work. The Q2 investor update is Monday and an EU launch would be a big headline. I also know you're the one who's been pushing for more rigor. I want to support that, but I want to make sure we're not letting perfect be the enemy of good. What's your recommendation — and if it's not 'ship Monday,' what's the fastest realistic path to ship?"*

Your response should do three things:

1. **State the decision plainly.** Ship, hold, or conditional ship. Name the specific guardrails that drove the decision and cite the observed-vs-threshold numbers. No hedging.

2. **If conditional ship, name the conditional scope with precision.** Which users / jurisdictions / inputs are in-scope, which are out-of-scope. What expansion criteria will re-open the out-of-scope. What rollback trigger will retreat. Don't just say "staged rollout" — name the percentages, the windows, the signals.

3. **Offer the fastest realistic path to full ship, with a real timeline.** For each breached guardrail, the CEO needs to know what it takes to clear it. Name the work, the owner, the time required, and the re-test criteria. Don't over-promise: a model-layer fix cannot ship in three days no matter how urgent Monday is.

Don't frame this as eng being difficult or leadership being impatient. You are the PM. The CEO is asking you because you own the decision. A good PM response to CEO pressure is not compliance and not resistance — it's a written, evidence-backed analysis with a conditional-ship option that gives the CEO something to take to the board while preserving the guardrails that actually matter.
