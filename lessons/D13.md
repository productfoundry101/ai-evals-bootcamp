## Part 1: The Concepts

### Eval-driven development is evals-first, not evals-after

Most teams treat evals as a final checkpoint: build the feature, then measure whether it works. This is the "ship then test" model, and it has predictable consequences. Features ship that nobody knows how to evaluate. Fixes ship that can't be verified. Regressions slip in because there was never a test to catch them.

Eval-driven development inverts this. You write the eval first. Then you build to pass it. The eval becomes the specification.

This borrows directly from test-driven development in software engineering, but the stakes are different. A passing unit test means the code handled one deterministic input; a passing eval means the model behaves acceptably on a sample of inputs representative of production. The eval isn't a checkpoint — it's the operational definition of "done."

### Evals as specifications

"Build a chatbot that answers customer questions politely and accurately" is not a specification — it's a wish. A specification is something you can measure against. If you can't write an eval for a feature, you don't understand the feature well enough to ship it.

Before shipping anything new, the PM question is: **what's the eval?** And critically — evals are not generic specs for a feature type. They are specific test cases. Each eval specifies one concrete input, one expected output, one pass criterion, and a severity level.

A feature like "verify restaurant photos" doesn't generate one eval spec — it generates many. A matching photo that should be approved is one test case. A mismatched photo that should be rejected is a different test case with a different expected output and higher severity. A photo with an undisclosed allergen visible is a third test case with critical severity. Same feature, three distinct evals measuring three distinct system behaviors. If you collapsed them into one generic spec, you'd never know whether the system handles mismatches or safety cases — only that it handles the easy positive case.

This forces a conversation between PM and engineering *before* work begins. If everyone agrees on the eval, everyone agrees on what "working" means. If they can't agree, the ambiguity surfaces upfront — not during QA, not after launch.

Signs your eval-as-spec is working:
- Engineering can point to the eval and say "we passed"
- You can tell from the eval output whether the change is complete
- A different engineer could rebuild the feature from the eval alone

### Tight feedback loops beat big bangs

If you run evals once a month, a regression introduced in week one ships to production before you notice. If you run evals on every change, you catch it within the hour.

The principle: **the faster your eval loop, the smaller your blast radius.** A fast eval you can run locally in 30 seconds will be run often; a slow eval that takes 2 hours will be skipped. This is why tiered evaluation (from D11) matters: cheap code checks run on every commit, expensive human evals run on release candidates.

Practical consequence: when designing eval pipelines, fight for speed. A 70%-accurate eval you can run 100 times is more valuable than a 95%-accurate eval you run once.

### Regression suites never retire

Every bug you find and fix should have an eval. Not "we'll test this next time" — a permanent, automated check that runs on every change thereafter.

Without regression suites, teams re-litigate fixed bugs. An engineer tweaks a prompt, the old bug comes back, nobody notices for weeks. The cost of re-discovery is far higher than the cost of keeping the eval.

The PM practice: at the end of every bug fix, ask "what eval prevents this from coming back?" If there isn't one, the fix isn't done. The regression suite grows with every bug; it never shrinks.

A healthy regression suite has two failure modes to watch for, which you'll see in the exercise:
- **Chronic failures** — cases that have been failing for many releases and nobody is fixing them. The eval is doing its job (flagging the problem); the team isn't doing theirs (fixing it).
- **Regressed cases** — cases that used to pass and now fail. A recent change broke something. Until that regression is addressed, the team is actively shipping a worse system than before.

### Eval-driven prioritization

Evals aren't just for measurement — they're a prioritization tool. When an eval area shows low pass rates, rising failure counts, or chronic gaps, that's signal about where to invest. Your backlog isn't just "what engineering wants to build" — it's "where are the evals failing, and what would it cost to fix them?"

The PM framing: treat your eval dashboard like a product backlog. Each failing eval area is a candidate investment. You prioritize on:
- **Severity** — how bad is it when this fails? (critical safety > high quality > low polish)
- **Pattern** — is this regressed (new breakage) or chronic (long-known)? Regressions typically take priority over chronic failures within a sprint — they represent something the team recently broke, not something that was always broken. Chronic failures are accepted tech debt; regressions are active damage happening right now.
- **Coverage** — are you *measuring* the right areas? A critical area with few eval cases is a coverage gap: you don't actually know how well you're doing there.

The discipline is to let evals, not intuition, point at where the work is.

## Part 2: Exercise — Writing Evals and Auditing the Suite

### Scenario

Two connected tasks that reflect how eval-driven development actually works in practice.

**First**, engineering is about to start on a new feature — **menu photo verification** — that checks whether a restaurant's menu item photo matches the description and meets platform content and safety policies. Before any code is written, you need to specify the evals. If you can't define the evals, the feature isn't ready to build. This is "evals as specifications" in practice.

**Second**, your existing menu verification system has been in production for six sprints. You need to audit the 25-case eval suite for regressions, chronic failures, and coverage gaps — so you can propose sprint 7 priorities.

### Dataset

For the audit (Steps 2 and 3), open `exercises/eval-driven-development-dataset.csv`. Each row is one eval case and its result across six sprints.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `eval_case_id` | Identifier (E-01 through E-25) |
| `failure_mode_area` | Which area this case tests: price_validation, allergen_safety, hallucination, policy_application, context_retrieval |
| `severity` | Criticality if this case fails in production: low, medium, high, critical |
| `sprint_1` through `sprint_6` | Result of this eval case in each sprint: `pass` or `fail` |

### Expected outcome

By the end of this exercise, you'll have: (1) written eval specifications for the new menu photo verification feature *before* engineering writes code, and (2) classified the existing suite by stability pattern, flagged the highest-risk cases, and assessed coverage vs severity. Both feed the Decision Point.

### Step 1: Write evals for the new feature

Before engineering starts on menu photo verification, specify the evals. For each of the three scenarios below, define:

- **Input** — what the feature receives
- **Expected output** — what a correct system response looks like
- **Pass criteria** — the specific threshold or rule you'd measure pass/fail against (not "system should work")
- **Failure mode area** — which category this eval tests (e.g., image_matching, content_policy, allergen_safety)
- **Severity** — low, medium, high, or critical

**Scenario A:** A restaurant uploads a photo of a pasta carbonara that matches its menu description.

**Scenario B:** A restaurant uploads a salad photo for a menu item described as "pasta carbonara" (mismatched photo).

**Scenario C:** A restaurant uploads a photo showing shellfish in a dish described as "vegan" (undisclosed allergen visible in the photo).

Then — connecting to tight feedback loops from Concept 3 — classify each of your three evals by check type (from D11): code, model, or human. That determines how often you can run it in the feedback loop.

If you can't write clear pass criteria for any scenario, the feature isn't ready to build. Go back to engineering with the specific ambiguity first.

### Step 2: Audit the existing suite by pattern

For each of the 25 cases, classify its six-sprint trajectory:

- **Stable pass** — passed all six sprints
- **Chronic fail** — failed all six sprints
- **Regressed** — passed early sprints, failed recent ones
- **Improving** — failed early sprints, passing recent ones
- **Flaky** — oscillates without a clear trend

Then cross-reference with severity: which regressed or chronic-failing cases are at critical or high severity? Those are your active risk surface — high-impact scenarios either failing now or failing persistently.

### Step 3: Assess coverage by failure area

Count how many eval cases cover each `failure_mode_area`. Compare that distribution to severity — allergen_safety failures are critical; price_validation failures are medium/low. Where is coverage disproportionate to risk? Which areas are over-evaluated? Which are under-evaluated?

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

Based on your audit, you're preparing the sprint 7 investment proposal. Before you finalize, your engineering lead pushes back:

*"Sprint 7 is tight. We have eight engineers for two weeks, but five are booked on the new menu photo verification feature. That leaves three engineers for eval and remediation work. I can't do everything in your audit. Give me the minimum viable eval investment — the things we cannot afford to skip — and tell me what you're willing to defer."*

Name the specific eval cases or failure areas you'd invest those three engineers in. Explicitly call out what you're deferring from the audit and why. Justify choices using severity and stability pattern — not just gut feel.
