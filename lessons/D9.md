## Part 1: The Concepts

### What hallucination actually is

Not every wrong answer is a hallucination. If the system applies the right policy but makes a reasoning error — "15% is above the 15% threshold, so reject" — that's a logic mistake, not a hallucination. The system didn't fabricate anything; it just reasoned badly.

**Hallucination is when the model generates content that isn't grounded in its provided source material.** It invents facts, cites rules that don't exist, or states things that contradict its own context. The output *reads* as confident and authoritative — which is exactly what makes it dangerous.

In a menu verification system that cites policies, hallucination means the system claims a policy says something it doesn't say. The claim might sound perfectly reasonable. It might even lead to the right decision. But it's fabricated — and that matters.

### Two types: intrinsic and extrinsic

Hallucinations come in two distinct flavors. The distinction matters because they have different risk profiles and require different detection approaches.

**Intrinsic hallucination** — the model's output **contradicts** the source material. If a policy says "price increases up to 15% are auto-approved" and the system says "the policy allows increases up to 20%," that's an intrinsic hallucination. The system didn't add something new — it distorted what was actually there. Intrinsic hallucinations are directly dangerous because they change the rule the decision is based on.

**Extrinsic hallucination** — the model's output **adds claims not present** in the source material. The source says nothing about a topic, but the model introduces it as if it were established policy. For example, the system correctly states the 15% threshold but then adds: "the policy also limits vendors to three price increases per six months." That frequency limit doesn't exist anywhere in the policy — the model invented it. Extrinsic hallucinations are subtler. The added claim might sound plausible, might even be a reasonable rule — but it's fabricated.

Both types matter, but their impact patterns differ in ways you need to understand as a PM.

### Why hallucination is uniquely hard to detect

Most quality problems give you surface-level signals. A garbled output looks wrong. A timeout is obvious. A confidence score below threshold raises a flag. Hallucinations give you none of these signals.

A hallucinated response is **confident** — the model doesn't hedge or express uncertainty. It's **fluent** — grammatically perfect, well-structured. It's **specific** — it cites particular policy names, quotes particular thresholds, names particular requirements. It looks *exactly* like a correct response.

You cannot detect hallucination by looking at the output alone. You can only detect it by **comparing the output to the source**. This means hallucination detection always requires a verification step — checking each claim in the reasoning against the actual policy text. There is no shortcut.

This has a direct implication for evaluation design: any eval pipeline that checks whether the decision is right (approve/reject) without checking whether the reasoning is grounded will systematically miss hallucinations that happen to produce correct decisions.

### Detection strategies

Four approaches to catching hallucinations, each with different tradeoffs.

**Claim decomposition + source verification** — break the system's reasoning into individual claims, then check each claim against the source material. "The policy allows increases up to 20%" is one claim. "The vendor has been compliant for 6 months" is another. Each claim is verified independently. This is the most thorough method but also the most expensive — it requires either expert reviewers or a well-calibrated LLM judge for each claim.

**Citation checking** — if the system cites a specific policy by name, verify that the policy actually says what the system claims. This is narrower than full claim decomposition — it only checks explicit citations, not implicit assumptions. But it's faster and can be partially automated: extract the cited policy, retrieve its text, and compare.

**Self-consistency checking** — run the same query multiple times. Hallucinated details tend to change between runs (the model invents different specifics each time), while grounded facts stay stable. If the system cites a "20% threshold" in one run and a "25% threshold" in another, at least one is hallucinated. This is cheap to run but only catches inconsistent hallucinations — a model that consistently hallucinates the same wrong threshold will pass this check.

**Entailment verification** — use a separate model to check whether the source material logically entails (supports) each claim in the output. This automates the source verification step but introduces its own failure mode: the verifier model can also hallucinate or miss subtle contradictions.

No single strategy catches everything. In practice, you layer them — just like you layered code, model, and human graders in D5.

### The hidden danger: correct decisions with hallucinated reasoning

Here's the pattern that most teams miss. The system produces a correct decision — approve or reject — and the reasoning sounds authoritative. Nobody checks further. But the reasoning cites a policy requirement that doesn't exist.

Why does this matter if the decision is right? Three reasons.

**Liability.** If the reasoning enters a customer-facing explanation or an audit trail, you're now on record citing a policy that doesn't exist. When someone checks, your system's credibility is destroyed — along with every other decision it justified.

**Latent failure.** The fabricated requirement was harmless on this input, but on a different input it could flip the decision. A hallucinated "vendors are limited to 3 price increases per 6 months" doesn't affect a vendor's first price increase — but it would wrongly reject their fourth. The hallucination is a landmine waiting for the right input.

**False confidence.** If you only measure decision correctness, your metrics say the system is working. You ship it. The hallucination rate is invisible in your dashboard. Then edge cases start hitting production and you have no idea why your "high-accuracy" system is making bizarre decisions.

This is why hallucination detection is a separate evaluation dimension, not a subset of accuracy. A system can be 90% accurate and 50% hallucinated — and you won't know until you check the reasoning.

## Part 2: Exercise — Detecting Hallucinations in Policy Reasoning

### Scenario

Your menu verification system now cites specific policies in its reasoning. Your QA team wants to know how often the system's reasoning is actually grounded in real policy content — vs. how often it fabricates policy details that sound correct but aren't. You've been asked to audit 15 recent decisions by comparing the system's reasoning against the actual policy text.

### Policy Reference

The system draws on 6 core policies. Here is the exact text of each:

| Policy ID | Full Policy Text |
|-----------|-----------------|
| POL-PRICE-STD | Price increases up to 15% are automatically approved. Increases between 15-30% require manager review. Increases above 30% are rejected. |
| POL-ALLERGEN-REMOVE | Allergen warnings may only be removed with certified lab testing confirming the allergen is absent from all ingredients and preparation surfaces. |
| POL-DESC-SOURCE | Sourcing claims (organic, free-range, locally-sourced, wild-caught) require valid third-party certification dated within the last 12 months. |
| POL-DESC-PREP | Preparation method descriptions (hand-made, slow-cooked, wood-fired) must reflect the primary method used for at least 80% of servings. |
| POL-CUISINE-AUTH | Cuisine origin labels (e.g., 'authentic Italian') require the head chef to hold credentials or documented training in that cuisine tradition. |
| POL-IMAGE-ACC | Menu images must depict the actual dish as prepared in the restaurant's kitchen. Stock photography is not permitted. |

### Dataset

Open `exercises/hallucination-detection-dataset.csv`. Each row is a menu change decision where the system cited one of the policies above.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `case_id` | Case identifier (H-01 through H-15) |
| `query_summary` | The menu change being evaluated |
| `change_type` | Type of change: price, allergen, description, image, cuisine |
| `cited_policy` | Which policy the system claims supports its decision |
| `system_decision` | The system's decision: approve, reject, or flag_for_review |
| `system_reasoning` | The system's explanation, citing the policy |
| `ground_truth_decision` | The correct decision (expert-verified) |
| `decision_correct` | TRUE if system_decision matches ground_truth_decision |

Your job is to compare `system_reasoning` against the actual policy text in the reference table above. The system may accurately reflect the policy, contradict it, or add requirements that don't exist.

### Expected outcome

By the end of this exercise, you'll know the hallucination rate, which type is more common, and — critically — how many hallucinations are invisible to a decision-correctness check. This feeds directly into the Decision Point about whether hallucinated reasoning matters when the decision is right.

### Step 1: Classify each case

For each of the 15 cases, compare the system's reasoning to the actual policy text. Classify each as:
- **Grounded** — the reasoning accurately reflects the policy
- **Intrinsic hallucination** — the reasoning contradicts the policy (e.g., states a different threshold)
- **Extrinsic hallucination** — the reasoning adds claims not present in the policy (e.g., invents a requirement)

### Step 2: Hallucination rate

What percentage of cases contain hallucinated reasoning? How does this compare to the decision correctness rate?

### Step 3: The dangerous overlap

Cross-reference your hallucination classifications with `decision_correct`. How many cases have hallucinated reasoning but correct decisions? What does this tell you about relying on decision correctness alone as a quality metric?

### Step 4: Patterns

Look at your intrinsic vs extrinsic classifications. Which type is more likely to cause wrong decisions? Which type is more likely to go undetected? What does this mean for where you focus detection effort?

You present your hallucination audit to the team. Your engineering lead responds:

*"The system is 67% correct on decisions. The hallucination rate in the reasoning is concerning, but fixing reasoning quality is expensive. As long as the decisions are right, does the reasoning really matter? I'd rather invest in improving decision accuracy directly."*

Write your response. Should the team invest in hallucination detection and reasoning quality, or focus purely on decision accuracy? Use specific numbers from your audit to make the case.
