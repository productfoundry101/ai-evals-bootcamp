## Part 1: The Concepts

### AI experiments have variance from sources traditional A/B tests don't

A traditional A/B test has one main source of variance: users. You randomize users into treatment and control, measure their behavior, compare means.

AI experiments add three more sources:

1. **Model stochasticity** — the same input produces different outputs across runs. Temperature, sampling, decoding randomness. Run the same test case 10 times, you might see 7 passes and 3 fails.
2. **Test case selection** — which cases you chose for the eval affects the outcome. Swap 5 of 20 cases and the measured accuracy can move by 10 points even if the system didn't change.
3. **Prompt and context variation** — small wording changes, retrieved context variations, and pipeline state produce different outputs on "the same" input.

Traditional A/B tests ignore these because they don't exist in deterministic software. AI experiments that ignore them produce results that don't replicate. A "5% improvement" that disappears on rerun is the most common way AI experiments mislead.

The consequence: **you need to report variance, not just means.** A single-number comparison ("v2 scores 75%, v1 scores 70%, ship v2") is the signature of a PM who hasn't internalized the non-determinism.

### Always report confidence intervals, never point estimates

The correct framing isn't "our system scores 85%" — it's "our system scores 85% ± 3%." That ±3% is the standard error around the mean.

Why this matters for ship decisions: if v1 scores 70% ± 5% and v2 scores 75% ± 5%, the confidence intervals overlap heavily. You cannot conclude that v2 is better. Reporting only the point estimates makes the comparison look decisive when it isn't.

The math is simple. Under the Central Limit Theorem, with n test cases and observed pass rate p:
- Standard error (SEM) ≈ √(p × (1 − p) / n)
- 95% confidence interval ≈ mean ± 1.96 × SEM

For a system scoring 75% on 40 test cases: SEM ≈ √(0.75 × 0.25 / 40) ≈ 0.068, so 95% CI ≈ ±13 percentage points. That's a huge range. Your "75%" could be anywhere from 62% to 88% if you resampled cases.

The PM discipline: **never accept a point-estimate comparison as evidence of improvement.** Ask for confidence intervals. If CIs overlap, the observed difference is within noise.

### Paired testing beats independent testing — always

Here's the most underused technique in AI experiments. When comparing v1 and v2, you can either:

- **Independent comparison:** run v1 on set A, run v2 on set B, compare means. (What most teams default to.)
- **Paired comparison:** run both v1 and v2 on the *same* test cases, then analyze the differences case by case.

Paired testing eliminates test-case-difficulty as a source of variance. Some cases are inherently harder than others. In independent comparison, that difficulty noise dominates. In paired comparison, you look at which cases v2 got right that v1 got wrong, and vice versa — and ignore the cases where both agreed.

Anthropic's research found that frontier models correlate 0.3 to 0.7 on which questions they get right or wrong. That correlation is exactly what paired testing exploits. In practice, paired tests detect differences 2–3x smaller than what independent tests can detect with the same sample size.

The PM rule: **run v1 and v2 on the same test cases and compare pairwise.** Looking at only the cases where the two versions disagree is where the signal lives.

### Sample size is a PM decision, not a given

"We tested 20 cases" is not a sample size — it's a statement. The real question is: 20 cases is enough to detect *what*?

Power analysis answers this. Given your test set size, what's the smallest real improvement you can reliably detect? If your detection floor is ±10 percentage points and the improvement you're chasing is 3 points, your experiment is pre-committed to inconclusiveness. You'll either get a false positive (lucky noise) or a false negative (real improvement lost in the static).

Two practical consequences:

1. **Before running an experiment, decide the smallest effect that matters.** If shipping v2 requires a 5-point improvement to justify cost, and your test set can only detect 10-point changes, stop and scale up the test set before running.
2. **Watch for shared context inflating your apparent sample size.** If 5 of your 40 test cases are menu items from the same restaurant, those 5 cases are more similar to each other than to the rest of the dataset — they share the same kitchen, same pricing style, same error patterns. Statistically, they count as roughly 1 independent data point, not 5. If you don't account for this and treat all 40 cases as fully independent, your confidence intervals will look tighter than they really are — and you may declare a result significant when it isn't. The fix: count your unique contexts (unique restaurants, unique conversations), not just your total cases. That's your real effective sample size.

For AI experiments, 20–40 cases is usually too small. 200+ cases with clustering handled correctly is where credible comparisons live.

### Aggregate improvements can hide subgroup regressions

Traditional A/B tests usually measure one primary metric. AI experiments should never. The non-determinism and the distribution-sensitivity of AI systems mean that the same change can improve the mean and catastrophically regress a subgroup.

You saw this in D12 (fairness): aggregate 78% approval rate hid Thai cuisine at 44%. The same mechanism plays out in experiments. A new prompt that nets +5 points on aggregate accuracy might be getting +15 points on common cases and −20 points on edge cases that matter most.

The discipline from D10 applies: every experiment needs a **primary metric plus a set of guardrails**. The primary metric is what you're optimizing. The guardrails are the regressions you refuse to accept, no matter how good the primary metric looks. Common guardrails for AI experiments: subgroup performance floors, latency/cost ceilings, safety-critical failure counts.

An experiment where the primary metric improves but a guardrail breaks is a **do-not-ship**, not a "ship and fix later." The mean looks good because the failures are concentrated in the minority you're not measuring.

## Part 2: Exercise — Analyzing an AI A/B Experiment

### Scenario

Engineering ran a two-week prompt experiment on the menu verification system. The current production prompt (v1) was compared against a revised prompt (v2) designed to be more cautious about ambiguous allergen disclosures. Both versions were run on the same 40-case evaluation set.

Engineering sent you a one-line summary: *"v2 scores 75%, v1 scores 65%. +10 points, let's ship."* You need to decide whether that summary is trustworthy before committing to the ship.

### Dataset

Open `exercises/ai-experiment-dataset.csv`. Each row is one test case evaluated on both versions.

| Column | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| `case_id` | Identifier (C-01 through C-40) |
| `cuisine` | Cuisine type: Thai, American, Indian |
| `restaurant_size` | Small or Large |
| `restaurant_id` | Which restaurant the test case came from (some restaurants contribute multiple cases) |
| `v1_result` | v1 prompt result on this case: pass or fail |
| `v2_result` | v2 prompt result on this case: pass or fail |

### Expected outcome

By the end: (1) you'll have computed aggregate pass rates *with confidence intervals* for both versions, (2) done a paired comparison to see whether the observed difference is real, (3) sliced by subgroup to find any hidden regressions, (4) assessed whether the sample size was adequate and whether clustering was handled.

### Step 1: Compute aggregate pass rates with confidence intervals

Count v1 passes and v2 passes out of 40. Compute each version's pass rate, SEM, and 95% CI.

Do the two confidence intervals overlap? If they do, what does that imply about engineering's "+10 points, ship it" claim?

### Step 2: Paired comparison — find the real signal

Independent comparison (Step 1) is the wrong tool. Use the pairing. For each case, there are four possible outcomes:

- Both v1 and v2 passed
- Both v1 and v2 failed
- v1 passed, v2 failed (v2 **regressed** this case)
- v1 failed, v2 passed (v2 **improved** this case)

Count each bucket. The cases where both agreed are non-informative — they cancel out. The signal lives in the disagreements.

Compute: (improved cases − regressed cases) / 40. This is the net paired improvement. How does it compare to the naive +10pp? Is the paired comparison more or less certain than the aggregate comparison, and why?

### Step 3: Slice by subgroup — find hidden regressions

Split the paired results by cuisine. For each cuisine, count improved vs regressed cases.

Is the aggregate +10pp improvement uniformly distributed, or is it the net of large improvements in some cuisines and regressions in others? If you find a cuisine where v2 regressed, what does that imply for the ship decision — regardless of what the aggregate says?

Bonus: cross-slice by restaurant_size if time permits.

### Step 4: Assess sample size and clustering

Two questions:

1. **Clustering:** How many unique `restaurant_id` values are in the dataset? If some restaurants contribute multiple cases, the 40 cases are not 40 independent observations. Estimate roughly how many effective independent clusters you actually have.

2. **Power:** For a subgroup with, say, n=10 cases (like the smallest cuisine group), what's the detection floor? Use SEM ≈ √(0.5 × 0.5 / 10) ≈ 0.16, so 95% CI ≈ ±31pp. Is any subgroup-level regression you detected in Step 3 credibly real given that floor, or could it be noise?

## Part 3: PM Decision Point

Based on your analysis, you have to respond to engineering's ship proposal. They push back:

*"You're overcomplicating this. Aggregate went from 65% to 75%. That's a 10-point improvement on the same 40 cases. What's the issue? Let's ship v2 and iterate if we see problems in production."*

Your response should do three things:

1. **Directly answer the ship question:** ship v2, hold v2, or ship v2 conditionally. Name the condition if you pick the third.
2. **Name at least two statistical issues** engineering's one-line summary glosses over. Use specifics from your analysis (aggregate CIs, paired comparison, subgroup splits, clustering, detection floor).
3. **Propose the follow-up experiment design** you'd actually trust. How many cases? What subgroup coverage? What guardrails? What would you do differently from this experiment?

Don't restate the concepts abstractly. Cite numbers you computed. If you don't have numbers for a claim, you haven't done the analysis.
