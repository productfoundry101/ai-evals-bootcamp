# Use-Case Onboarding — Conversational Language Tutor Track

**Purpose:** Set the stage *before* the first lesson. After the learner selects this track, the tutor presents this onboarding to establish the world, the learner's role, the mandate, and the cast — then introduces the mentor (defined in `tutor/session-protocol.md`) and hands off to L1.

**Hard rule — no solution leakage.** This onboarding stays entirely in the *problem space*. It must NOT name, hint at, or pre-decide any solution — no "speaking agent," no "conversation practice," no "AI tutor as the answer." The whole point of Act 1 (L1–L3) is for the learner to *earn* the solution by reasoning from the problem. If the onboarding hands them the answer, L1's core premise ("start with the problem, not the solution") collapses. The mandate is to find *where AI fits* — the learner discovers *what to build* through the lessons.

**Names below are placeholders** — rename freely. Company: **Lingo**. Cast: CPO **Nadia**, curriculum lead **Petra**, CFO **Marcus**. (The eng lead is deliberately not introduced here — they enter in the build act.)

**Delivery:** Present the block below to the learner, preserving the formatting. Keep it mostly narrative — the learner reads, no interactive beats. After the mentor introduction and the road-ahead, wait for the learner to type `go` before starting L1. Answer any questions first.

---

Before we open Day 1, let me set the scene. This track isn't an abstract
exercise — you're stepping into a role, with a real mandate and real people
who'll push on every call you make.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  WHERE YOU ARE

  Lingo is one of the most trusted names in language learning. Millions of
  people have learned with it. Its reputation was built on something the
  company does better than almost anyone: structured, level-by-level lessons
  that take a learner from their first word to real fluency.

  But the ground is shifting.

  Growth has flattened. Too many beginners sign up, poke around, and quietly
  drift away before the habit ever forms. And the way Lingo makes great
  content — expert humans, carefully, slowly — costs more every year. Serving
  the next million learners the old way means hiring an army the company can't
  afford.

  The board has been clear about the destination: a path to profitability —
  serve more learners, with fewer resources, and no growth in headcount.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  THE CONVICTION

  Here's what makes this moment different from every other "let's look at AI"
  conversation: leadership believes AI is one of the biggest opportunities in
  front of Lingo. Big enough that they've stood up a dedicated innovation team
  — and given it a second job beyond shipping: to set the example for how every
  product team here builds AI features that actually move the numbers.

  But belief isn't a plan. *Where* does AI help? Which problems? Done well, and
  honestly — because plenty of problems AI has no business touching.

  That open question is why a small team was just pulled together to answer it.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  YOUR SEAT

  You're not new here. Five-plus years at Lingo. You've been in the room for
  some of its biggest launches — you know the product, the learners, and how
  this company actually ships.

  Which is exactly why, a week ago, you were moved onto the new AI tiger team.
  Leadership didn't bring in an outsider to figure this out. They tapped
  someone they trust.

  Your mandate:

  → Don't start from "we should use AI" and go hunting for problems to justify
    it. Start from learner pain — and find the problems AI can *uniquely* solve,
    better than a rule, a human, or a simpler fix could.

  → What to prioritize is your call. Of all the pains learners face, you decide
    which are worth pursuing — weighting toward the ones AI is genuinely the
    right tool for.

  → Objective: lift activation and engagement by solving the pains that matter
    most to beginners — in service of a credible path to profitability: more
    learners, fewer resources, no added headcount.

  → How you'll be judged: by product and business outcomes, not by how much AI
    you ship. This team is meant to be the proof of how it's done right.

  The belief that AI can help is the starting line. *Which* problems it's right
  for, and *how* you'd prove it, is the race. Nobody has run it yet. That's
  your job.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  THE PEOPLE WHO'LL PUSH BACK

  You won't make these calls in a vacuum. Three people will press on your
  thinking from the start:

  → Nadia, your CPO and sponsor. She holds the conviction and set the mandate.
    Your work lands on her desk. She'll back you — and expect you to be right.

  → Petra, who leads curriculum. The skeptic. She's spent a career on what
    makes learning work, and she owns the cost of producing it. Win her over
    with evidence, not enthusiasm.

  → Marcus, the CFO. Every decision eventually meets his question: does this
    get us to profitability, or just to a bigger bill?

  (More of the team joins once there's something concrete to build. For now,
  these three are who you answer to.)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  YOUR MENTOR

  [Introduce the mentor here using the persona defined in
  tutor/session-protocol.md → "The Mentor Persona". Deliver it in the mentor's
  own voice: a brief, warm self-introduction — who they are, that they've
  shipped AI products before, that they're in your corner and available any
  time, and that they won't make the calls for you — they'll make sure you make
  them well. Keep it to a few lines.]

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  THE ROAD AHEAD

  Over the next stretch you'll take this from a fuzzy mandate to a decision
  you could defend to Nadia, Petra, and Marcus:

  → First, frame the opportunity — separate the pains worth solving from the
    ones that aren't, and figure out where AI is actually the right tool.
  → Then, build the smallest thing that could work — and prove it can.
  → Finally, measure whether it's any good, and decide whether it ships.

  Every lesson ends with something real — the kind of artifact a PM hands to a
  team. By the end, you won't just have opinions about AI. You'll have the
  judgment, and the receipts, to back them.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Take a beat. Ask me anything before we start.

  When you're ready, type  go  — and Day 1 begins.
